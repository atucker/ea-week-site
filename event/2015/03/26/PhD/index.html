Pitch Your PhD: Research at the Intersection of Effectiveness and Altruism

Harvard Candidates PhD Panel
March 26
7 PM  ⁄  Sever Hall 306
RSVP

Find out what it’s like to pursue a path of changing the world through academia and get a chance to meet people who are doing it.

Young scholars from various departments and graduate schools at Harvard will pitch their research on the intersection of effectiveness and altruism.

Topics include: - How to make people behave more altruistically - What programs are most cost-effective - Theories that allow us to say what works and what doesn’t

The event will start with short presentations from the speakers, followed by break-out sessions according to interest area. This event is open to the public.

Speakers:

—Erez Yoeli, Visiting Scholar —Oliver Hauser, PhD Candidate —Moshe Hoffman, Research Scientist Harvard University Program for Evolutionary Dynamics

“Promoting Effective Altruism”: If you want people to focus on effectiveness, it’s not enough to just tell them about effectiveness–you must make effectiveness public.

—Anders Huitfeldt ScD Candidate, Department of Epidemiology Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health

“Correlation, Causation and Counterfactual Theory”: The epistemology of medical decision making, and how modern methods for causal inference can be used to learn about the safety and effectiveness of pharmaceuticals and other medical interventions.

—Guilherme Lichand PhD candidate in Political Economy and Government

“Evidence-based policy design”: From rainfall insurance to protect poor farmers from droughts, to random audits to prevent bureaucrats from embezzling federal transfers that support municipal health spending, in my work I show that it is often the case that such policies have unintended consequences, and that rigorously documenting their effects could better inform policy design.

—Mauricio Fernandez Duque PhD candidate in Political Economy and Government

“Helping those that can’t get by or those who live close by?”: I conducted a large scale field experiment in Mexico that shows the conditions in which supermarket customers contribute more to beneficiaries in their region, and how this bias is affected by an appeal to nationalism.
